Total climate finance has steadily increased over the last decade, reaching USD 632 billion in 2019/2020.

However, this is nowhere near enough finance to limit global warming to 1.5 Â°C.

Climate Policy Initiative has analyzed and aggregated scenarios that explore climate finance needs for energy systems, buildings, industry, transport, and other mitigation and adaptation solutions.

And using these future needs estimates, Climate finance must increase by at least 588% to USD 4.35 trillion by 2030 to meet our climate objectives.

This diagram provides a snapshot of climate finance flows through the lifecycle, as the thickness of the lines represents the amount of money flowing. CPI presents annual averages to smooth out fluctuations in the data, so the numbers here represent an annual average over 2019 and 2020.

The public sector together accounted for 51% (USD 321 billion) of 2019/2020 climate finance. Development Finance Institutions (DFIs) continued to provide the majority of public finance, contributing 68%.

Private actors accounted for 49% (USD 310 billion) of 2019/2020 climate finance, with commercial financial institutions and corporations the biggest players (together contributing 79% of the total).

The Landscape categorizes transactions by the instrument used to structure the provision of climate finance, covering debt, equity and grants.

Grants accounted for 6% (USD 36 billion) of 2019/2020 climate finance, with governments as the main source of grant funding.

Market-rate debt accounted for 88% of total climate finance debt. Over 99% of concessional, low-cost project debt was provided by public institutions.

Equity investments accounted for 33% (USD 206 billion) of total climate finance, a step up from 167 billion in 2017/2018.

A majority of climate finance went to mitigation (90%) while adaptation and projects with multiple objectives accounted for a much lower share of the total, at 7% and 2% respectively.

Nearly all adaptation finance tracked in the Landscape was funded by public actors (98%). Adaptation finance primarily flowed towards Water & Wastewater and Others & Cross-sectoral projects.

Dual use finance primarily flowed to Others & Cross sectoral projects, and 86% of the total was committed by public actors.

The majority of mitigation finance was committed for energy systems, which includes investments in renewable fuel production, renewable power and heat generation assets, transmission and distribution networks, as well as support to policy and national budget and capacity building. Private actors provided the majority of mitigation finance (54%), particularly in terms of the share of renewable energy finance.

The Landscape tracks and analyses climate finance along six broad sector classifications yielding adaptation, mitigation, or dual benefits: Water & Wastewater; Infrastructure & Industry; Others & Cross-sectoral; Land Use; Transport; and Energy Systems.

Water & Wastewater climate finance includes projects concerned with water supply & sanitation and wastewater treatment.

Infrastructure & Industry climate finance includes, among others, construction work, heating, ventilation & air conditioning, and industrial, extraction and manufacturing processes.

Others & Cross-sectoral climate finance covers miscellaneous projects including policy & national budget support & capacity building, biodiversity, land & marine conservation, and disaster-risk management.

Land Use climate finance includes projects concerned with agriculture, forestry & fisheries. Key activities include sustainable crops, afforestation & reforestation, and sustainable fish production.

Transport climate finance includes projects concerned with private road transport (e.g. EV chargers), rail & public transport, waterways, aviation, and transport-oriented infrastructure.

Energy Systems climate finance covers power & heat generation, transmission & distribution, and fuel production, transmission & distribution. Key activities include renewable energy production, energy efficiency schemes and infrastructure for resilience.
